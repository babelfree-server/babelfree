## KEY INSIGHT

Carroll's grammar rarely exceeds B1-B2 (simple past, conversational dialogue, short sentences), but the semantic/pragmatic/philosophical demands escalate sharply. Surface simplicity masks deep complexity — ideal for B2-C1 learners developing inferential and metalinguistic skills.

## LITERARY PEAKS — Best passages for course use

### B2 Peaks (accessible, quotable, powerful)

**LG-B2a. The Red Queen's running paradox (Ch.II)**
Lines ~750–777
> "Now, _here_, you see, it takes all the running _you_ can do, to keep in the same place. If you want to get somewhere else, you must run at least twice as fast as that!"
- Conditionals, comparative structures, iconic metaphor

**LG-B2b. The chessboard world (Ch.II)**
Lines 727–734
> "It's a great huge game of chess that's being played—all over the world"
- Passive voice, metaphor, accessible

**LG-B2c. The "Nobody" exchange (Ch.VII)**
Lines 2584–2588
> "I see nobody on the road," said Alice. "I only wish I had such eyes," the King remarked. "To be able to see Nobody! And at that distance, too!"
- Logic puzzle, reification of negation

**LG-B2d. Mutual belief bargain (Ch.VII)**
Lines 2805–2806
> "If you'll believe in me, I'll believe in you. Is that a bargain?"
- First conditional, philosophical compact

**LG-B2e. Flowers speaking (Ch.II)**
Lines 543–551
> "We _can_ talk," said the Tiger-lily: "when there's anybody worth talking to."
- Personification, B1-B2, charming and accessible

### C1 Peaks (philosophical, metalinguistic, emotionally deep)

**LG-C1a. Jabberwocky (Ch.I)**
Lines 445–478 (complete poem)
> "'Twas brillig, and the slithy toves / Did gyre and gimble in the wabe"
- Entirely invented lexicon with real English morphology. The C2 peak of the book.
- Alice's response (lines 481–485): "It seems to fill my head with ideas—only I don't exactly know what they are!" — metacognitive reflection on language.

**LG-C1b. The wood where things have no names (Ch.III)**
Lines 1130–1183
> Alice loses her name, befriends a Fawn who walks beside her. The Fawn flees the moment naming returns.
- Deeply moving meditation on language and innocence. Names create social categories.
- One of Carroll's masterpiece passages.

**LG-C1c. The Red King's dream (Ch.IV)**
Lines 1486–1528
> "You're only a sort of thing in his dream!"
- Ontological doubt. Reality vs. dream. Anticipates Borges.

**LG-C1d. Tweedledee's "logic" (Ch.IV)**
Lines 1252–1253
> "If it was so, it might be; and if it were so, it would be; but as it isn't, it ain't. That's logic."
- Conditional chain parodying formal reasoning. Subjunctive vs. indicative.

**LG-C1e. "Jam to-morrow" (Ch.V)**
Lines 1727–1735
> "The rule is, jam to-morrow and jam yesterday—but never jam to-day."
- Temporal logic puzzle. Cited in philosophy of language.

**LG-C1f. "A poor sort of memory" (Ch.V)**
Lines 1749–1750
> "It's a poor sort of memory that only works backwards."
- One of Carroll's most quoted lines. Reversed causality.

**LG-C1g. "Six impossible things before breakfast" (Ch.V)**
Lines 1854–1859
> "Why, sometimes I've believed as many as six impossible things before breakfast."
- Epistemology of belief. Iconic.

**LG-C1h. The Knight's farewell (Ch.VIII)**
Lines 3269–3279
> "Of all the strange things that Alice saw... this was the one that she always remembered most clearly. Years afterwards she could bring the whole scene back again..."
- The most emotionally resonant prose in the entire book. Carroll's farewell to Alice Liddell. Elegiac.

**LG-C1i. The name/called/is puzzle (Ch.VIII)**
Lines 3242–3262
> "The name of the song is called 'Haddocks' Eyes.'" / "That's what the name is _called_. The name really _is_ 'The Aged Aged Man.'"
- Four-level metalinguistic puzzle about reference, naming, and identity.

**LG-C1j. "The use of a child without any meaning" (Ch.IX)**
Lines 3481–3484
> "What do you suppose is the use of child without any meaning? Even a joke should have some meaning—and a child's more important than a joke, I hope."
- Carroll's philosophy of meaning, compressed.

---

### C2 Peaks (philosophy of language, metafiction, elegy)

**LG-C2a. "Which is to be master" (Ch.VI)**
Lines 2302–2309
> "When _I_ use a word, it means just what I choose it to mean—neither more nor less." / "The question is whether you _can_ make words mean so many different things." / "The question is which is to be master—that's all."
- Carroll's most cited passage. Philosophy of meaning. Anticipates Wittgenstein.

**LG-C2b. Portmanteau coinage (Ch.VI)**
Lines 2361–2363
> "'Slithy' means 'lithe and slimy.' You see it's like a portmanteau—there are two meanings packed up into one word."
- Created a linguistic term still in use today.

**LG-C2c. Verbs are the proudest (Ch.VI)**
Lines 2312–2315
> "They've a temper, some of them—particularly verbs, they're the proudest—adjectives you can do anything with, but not verbs"
- Metalinguistic allegory. Words as servants with personalities.

**LG-C2d. Who dreamed whom? (Ch.XII)**
Lines 4051–4061
> "He was part of my dream, of course—but then I was part of his dream, too! ... Which do you think it was?"
- Ontological paradox. Metafiction. Fourth-wall break to reader.

**LG-C2e. Final poem — "A boat beneath a sunny sky" (complete)**
Lines 4064–4090
> "Still she haunts me, phantomwise, / Alice moving under skies / Never seen by waking eyes."
> Final line: "Life, what is it but a dream?"
- Acrostic (ALICE PLEASANCE LIDDELL). Elegiac. Carroll's farewell to childhood itself.

**LG-C2f. Reversed causality — scream then prick (Ch.V)**
Lines 1795–1817
> The Queen screams BEFORE pricking her finger. Cause follows effect.
- Requires tracking events in anti-chronological order.
