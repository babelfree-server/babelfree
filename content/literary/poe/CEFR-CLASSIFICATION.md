## POEMS

### B1 — Simple vocabulary, clear imagery, musical rhythm, short

| # | Title | File | Lines | Theme | Notes |
|---|-------|------|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 | **Eldorado** | poetical-works | 1876–1907 | quest | 4 stanzas, 24 lines. Shadow as guide. Simple vocabulary, strong rhythm. The easiest Poe poem — a knight seeking Eldorado, told he must ride "Down the Valley of the Shadow." Quest allegory a child could follow. |
| 2 | **Annabel Lee** | poetical-works | 1517–1569 | love/death | 6 stanzas, 41 lines. Ballad rhythm, "kingdom by the sea" refrain. Simple vocabulary (child, love, angels, wind, sea). His last completed poem. One of the most accessible poems in English. |

| 5 | **To My Mother** | poetical-works | 1652–1674 | family | Sonnet, 14 lines. "The angels... / Can find... none so devotional as that of 'Mother.'" Clear sentiment, sonnet form teachable. |
| 6 | **A Dream within a Dream** | poetical-works | 1957–1987 | philosophy | 24 lines, 2 stanzas. "All that we see or seem / Is but a dream within a dream." Famous. The sand-through-fingers image is visceral and concrete. Philosophical but grammatically simple. |
| 7 | **Alone** | poetical-works | 6597–6624 | identity | 22 lines. "From childhood's hour I have not been / As others were." Autobiographical, iconic. Simple grammar, profound isolation. Perfect for discussing identity. |

### B2 — Richer vocabulary, sustained metaphor, musical complexity

| # | Title | File | Lines | Theme | Notes |
|---|-------|------|-------|-------|-------|
| 8 | **The Raven** | poetical-works | 1019–1154 | grief/madness | 108 lines, 18 stanzas. The crown jewel. ### C1-C2

| # | Title | File | Lines | Theme | Notes |
|---|-------|------|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 | **The Philosophy of Composition** | poetical-works | 9286–9800 | craft | **THE capstone.** Poe dissects step-by-step how he wrote The Raven: choice of refrain ("Nevermore"), choice of speaker (a raven), choice of subject (death of a beautiful woman), choice of tone (melancholy), choice of metre (trochaic octameter). ## RECOMMENDED COURSE SEQUENCE

### The Poe Arc (B1 → C2)

**B1 — Gateway** (the hook):
1. "Eldorado" → quest, rhythm, 24 lines
2. "Annabel Lee" → love, ballad, refrain
3. "A Dream within a Dream" → philosophy in 24 lines
4. "Alone" → identity, autobiography
5. *The Tell-Tale Heart* → first tale, the heartbeat
6. *The Oval Portrait* → art kills, 900 words

**B2 — The Core** (where Poe takes hold):
7. "The Raven" → THE poem, with "Nevermore" refrain as scaffold
8. "The Bells" → sound as meaning, pronunciation masterclass
9. "To Helen" (1831) → Greece, Rome, perfection in 15 lines
10. "The Haunted Palace" → allegory of madness (before reading "Usher")
11. "The Conqueror Worm" → allegory of death (before reading "Ligeia")
12. *The Cask of Amontillado* → revenge, dialogue, carnival
13. *The Masque of the Red Death* → allegory, the seven rooms
14. *The Pit and the Pendulum* → survival, suspense, rescue
15. *Hop-Frog* → justice, the oppressed strike back

**C1 — The Masterworks** (immersion):
16. "Ulalume" → landscape as grief
17. "The City in the Sea" → apocalyptic vision
18. "Israfel" → art as divine calling
19. *The Fall of the House of Usher* → the gothic cathedral (contains "The Haunted Palace")
20. *Ligeia* → love beyond death (contains "The Conqueror Worm")
21. *The Murders in the Rue Morgue* → birth of the detective story
22. *William Wilson* → the doppelgänger
23. *The Gold-Bug* → cryptography, treasure, logic

**C2 — The Architect Revealed**:
24. **The Philosophy of Composition** → how The Raven was built, step by step
25. **The Poetic Principle** → Poe's theory of beauty
26. "Al Aaraaf" → Poe's cosmological vision
27. *The Narrative of A. Gordon Pym* → novel, for advanced independent reading

## KEY INSIGHTS

### Why Poe completes the English course trinity

| Author | Gives | CEFR sweet spot | Dimension |
|--------|-------|----------------|-----------|
| **Dickinson** | Compression, metaphysics, riddles | B1-C2 | The inner world |
| **Carroll** | Wordplay, logic, nonsense | B2-C2 | The playful world |
| **Poe** | Gothic, detection, craft | B1-C2 | The dark world + the architect's eye |

### The Philosophy of Composition as ultimate capstone
Where Dickinson's "A word is dead" asks what language IS, and Carroll's Looking-Glass asks what logic IS, Poe's essay asks: **how is a poem MADE?** It's the only work in the entire English course that teaches the student to think like a writer, not just a reader. The progression: feel the poem (B2, The Raven) → understand how it was engineered (C2, The Philosophy of Composition).

### Poe's embedded poems
Two poems live INSIDE tales:
- "The Haunted Palace" is recited by Roderick Usher in *The Fall of the House of Usher*
- "The Conqueror Worm" is recited by the narrator in *Ligeia*

These create a natural curriculum path: learn the poem at B2 → encounter it again embedded in the tale at C1. Spiral return.

### The Raven as structural center
The Raven sits at B2 — accessible enough to read, rich enough to study. Then at C2, The Philosophy of Composition dissects it completely. This creates the longest single-work arc in the English course: a poem met at B2 that is fully understood only at C2.
