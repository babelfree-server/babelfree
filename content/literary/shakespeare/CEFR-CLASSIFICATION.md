## KEY INSIGHTS

### Shakespeare's dimension in the English course
Where Poe is the architect, Dickinson the compressor, Whitman the expander, and Carroll the logician — Shakespeare is **the rhetorician**. He teaches argument, persuasion, the public voice. Every speech in Hamlet is an argument with itself.

### Sonnet XVIII as universal entry point
"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" is the most accessible great poem in English. Simple vocabulary, clear metaphor, famous. It IS the gateway to Shakespeare.

### "To be or not to be" as structural center
Like The Raven for Poe, this speech sits at B2 — accessible enough to attempt, rich enough to return to forever. The argumentative structure (question → catalogue → counter-argument → paralysis) teaches essay writing as much as literary analysis.

### Hamlet's soliloquies as a progression
The four major soliloquies map perfectly to CEFR:
- "O that this too too solid flesh" (C1) — raw emotion, classical allusion
- "O, what a rogue" (C1) — meta-theatrical self-analysis
- "To be or not to be" (B2) — philosophical but accessible
- "How all occasions" (C1) — the resolution of the action problem

Teaching them in chronological order IS the Hamlet course.

### The sonnets as a novel
The 154 sonnets tell a story: Fair Youth (1–126), Dark Lady (127–152), with Rival Poet disrupting. At C2, students can read the sequence as narrative, not just individual poems.
