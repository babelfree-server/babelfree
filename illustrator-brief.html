01 The Project

What you are illustrating and why it matters

El Viaje del Jaguar is an interactive language-learning game set in the ecosystems of Colombia. A female jaguar-spirit named Yaguará — who begins as a dreamer child and grows into a wise elder — travels through rainforest, Andes, llanos, desert, islands, snowy peaks, and Caribbean coast, meeting allies and facing forces of forgetting that are erasing the world's names. The game spans 58 destinations, 15,842 games across 8 ecosystems, from CEFR A1 to C2.

The visual world draws from Colombian indigenous art, textile patterns, and natural landscapes. It is NOT Disney, manga, or generic fantasy. These are spirits, not cartoon animals. This world is alive, warm, and ancient.

"No todas las palabras se dicen. Algunas se cuidan."

Visual Identity Keywords

Warm, earthy, alive Textile-inspired Organic shapes Circles & spirals Illustrated, not photorealistic Golden-hour light Mola patterns (Kuna) Wayúu woven textures Pre-Columbian gold motifs

What this is NOT

No Disney/Pixar style No anime/manga No generic fantasy No photorealistic 3D No clip art or flat corporate No AI-generated aesthetic No hard geometric edges No harsh/fluorescent light

02 The Three Worlds

Visual atmosphere and palette for each world

The game takes place across three visual worlds. Each has a distinct color palette, mood, and landscape. As the story progresses, the world shifts from intimate jungle to open terrain to cosmic sky.

El Mundo de Abajo

Jungle, river, night, roots.
Dense Chocó rainforest. Filtered green-gold light. Massive ceiba trees, buttress roots, rivers, bioluminescent moss. Warm and intimate — small spaces, close encounters. Fireflies, golden frogs, the sound of water everywhere.

Palette: deep greens, river blues, earth browns, night purples, gold accents.

El Mundo del Medio

Villages, mountains, plains, coast.
Colombia's four ecosystems in full. Chocó stilt-houses, Andean páramo, Orinoco grasslands, Caribbean shore. Human spaces mixed with nature — markets, paths, boats, abandoned buildings reclaimed by vines. Wider compositions, more characters, more complexity.

Palette: terracotta, ochre, mountain grey-blue, llanos gold, Caribbean turquoise.

El Mundo de Arriba

Stars, silence, light, transformation.
Cosmic and metaphysical. No solid walls — boundaries defined by constellations. Floors made of maps, ceilings made of sky. Luminous outlines rather than solid surfaces. Think: inside an astrolabe, a constellation chart come alive.

Palette: deep indigo, starlight silver, luminous amber, white-gold, cosmic black.

Yaguará grows across the game. She is NOT a fixed design — she has three distinct life stages mapped to the CEFR bands. Each stage must be visually recognizable at a glance, even at thumbnail size.

Fur Golden-light with faint, still-forming rosettes Golden-dark with sharp, clearly defined rosettes Deep gold-amber, dense rosettes, greying muzzle
Eyes Wide, bright green with amber flecks — everything is new Green-amber balanced, focused — she knows what she’s looking for Amber dominant, deep-set, calm — she has already seen it all

The literary authors — A1–C2 recurring characters, woven through the entire journey

Four real Colombian authors appear as magical-realist presences throughout Yaguará’s world, from A1 to C2. At A1, they are dreamlike — a figure appears, speaks a single line, glows, then dissolves. By B1+, they become full narrative characters with recurring literary peaks. By C1–C2, their works become the philosophical summit of each destination. The authors grow with the student across all 58 destinations.

Distribution: Pombo (16 appearances, A1–C2), Rivera (16, A1–C2), Carrasquilla (14, A1–C2), Macías (44, A1–C2 — dominant from B1+ as “El Maestro”, the philosophical spine of the game).

Visual rule: NOT photorealistic portraits. Stylized, warm, illustrated with the same organic line quality as the animal characters — but unmistakably human and recognizably based on the real authors’ actual appearances. Think Shaun Tan’s human figures or Remedios Varo’s dreamlike people. They exist between the real and the mythic. Reference the authors’ actual photographs for face, build, and bearing.

El Maestro — Luis Fernando Macías Zuluaga (b. 1957)
The Guide — Poet, narrator, professor — Contemporary (licensed) — Priority 1

El Maestro is the philosophical spine of the entire game. While the other three authors are recurring literary voices, Macías is the guide — the teacher who walks beside Yaguará from B1 onward. He appears in 44 games across B1–C2, making him the most frequent human presence in the game after Candelaria. His works (“La rana sin dientes”, “Los animales del cielo”, “La canción del barrio”) form the literary summit of every destination from dest19 forward. “Nombrar es crear” — his central teaching — is the game’s thesis.

  • Real appearance (see assets/reference/luis-fernando-macias.jpg): Late 60s. Short grey/white hair, slightly receding. Rectangular dark-framed glasses. Round, warm face with laugh lines — a man who smiles easily and fully. Light stubble. Broad, open smile. Plaid button-down shirt, casual and approachable. Not imposing — friendly, accessible. Stocky build.
  • Theme: Teaching, wisdom, language, creation through naming. “The word as a living thing.”
  • Setting: By the river with a book. The space around him feels like a classroom that became a garden. As the game progresses (B2–C2), he appears in more varied settings: mountain paths, starlit clearings, the ceiba’s roots.
  • Clothing: Modern but understated — clean button-down, simple trousers. By C-band, add a light ruana over his shoulders (echo of Carrasquilla, but contemporary).
  • Props: A book (open or closed), the river behind him. At C1+, he may hold a notebook with handwritten text visible. A pen always in his shirt pocket.
  • Key moments:
    • “Un hombre con lentes aparece junto al río. Tiene un libro.” (A1 — first appearance)
    • “Nombrar es crear. Cuando digas lo que será, lo estarás creando.” (B1 — the teaching)
    • “La rana sin dientes puede sostener palabras.” (C2 — the revelation)
  • Narrative arc: At A1, he appears briefly as a dreamlike figure. At B1, he becomes a recurring teacher — present but not dominant. At B2–C1, he is a full narrative character with dialogue, opinions, and emotional depth. At C2, he is the wise elder who steps back so the student can speak.

  • Explaining with quiet passion — open hands, eyes bright
  • Smiling at a student’s insight — genuine joy, pride, laugh lines deepening
  • Reading — eyes down, absorbed, one finger marking a page
  • Contemplative — looking at the river, glasses pushed up on forehead
  • Farewell — warm but resolute, the teacher releasing the student

Selva (Chocó / Amazon)

green, water, rain, drums — Guide: Colibrí — Afro-Colombian & indigenous cultures
  • Dense canopy, filtered green light, massive buttress roots, river visible below
  • Rain. Always a sense of rain, even if it's not actively raining. Wet leaves, mist, droplets.
  • Textile reference: Mola-inspired pattern border (Kuna/Guna layered appliqué)
  • This is Yaguará's HOME — it should feel warm, safe, and alive

Andes

earth, stone, cold clear light, wind instruments — Guide: Cóndor Viejo — Highland communities
  • Mountain valley, terraced fields (like Muisca agricultural terraces), stone paths
  • Cold clear light, long shadows, clouds below the viewer (condor territory)
  • Textile reference: Wayúu-inspired geometric pattern border
  • Feeling: ancient, vast, quiet. You can hear the wind.

Llanos

gold, open sky, harp, endless horizon — Guide: Delfín Rosado — Llanero culture
  • Vast golden grassland, enormous sky (sky should be 2/3 of the image), scattered moriche palms
  • River cutting through the plain, heat shimmer on the horizon
  • Textile reference: Sombrero vueltiao weave pattern border
  • Feeling: freedom, exposure, nowhere to hide. The opposite of the sheltered forest.

Costa (Caribbean)

turquoise, salt, cumbia, intense heat — Guide: Tortuga Marina — Afro-Caribbean culture
  • Turquoise water, mangroves at the edge, fishing boats (small, colorful, wooden)
  • Coral visible underwater, intense saturated light, cumbia energy
  • Textile reference: Filigree / gold-inspired pattern border (Momposina goldwork tradition)
  • Feeling: vibrant, rhythmic, warm. Life at the edge of land and sea.

Bosque (Cloud Forest / Andean Forest)

moss, mist, vertical green, bird calls — Transition between selva and Andes
  • Cloud forest: dense, vertical, dripping with epiphytes, orchids, bromeliads, hanging moss
  • Filtered light through mist — everything is soft-edged, almost dreamlike
  • Tree ferns, narrow muddy paths, hidden waterfalls, spectacled bears (suggested, not shown)
  • Textile reference: Arhuaco mochila weave pattern border (Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta)

La Búsqueda del Nombre Perdido — Riddle Quest Visual System

A 58-riddle quest woven through the entire game. One riddle per destination, escalating from A1 to C2. The quest follows La Rana sin Dientes (a frog who can hold words but cannot speak them) and is chronicled in El Cuaderno de Candelaria (a handwritten journal).

La Rana sin Dientes (The Toothless Frog)

  • What she is: A small golden frog — the same species that appeared in dest1 and went silent in dest6. She returns as the riddle quest’s mascot.
  • Visual: Golden skin, oversized eyes, NO teeth (her mouth is a smooth, gentle curve). She sits on words, holds them in her front paws, perches on the edges of Candelaria’s notebook.
  • Scale: Tiny. She fits in a human palm or on Yaguará’s paw.
  • Progression: At A1, she is translucent and fading. As the student solves riddles, she becomes more solid, more golden, more present. By C2, she glows.
  • Connection to Rinrín: She is NOT Rinrín (Pombo’s male frog). She is his counterpart — a female frog from Macías’s world. They are the dual frog arc of the game.

El Cuaderno de Candelaria (The Journal)

  • What it is: Candelaria’s notebook becomes the student’s riddle journal. Each solved riddle adds a handwritten entry.
  • Visual: Worn leather cover, pages sticking out at angles, pressed flowers and leaves between pages, ink stains, pencil sketches in the margins.
  • Interior pages: Lined paper with Candelaria’s handwriting (neat but youthful at A1, confident and flowing by C2).
  • Deliverables: Journal cover (closed + open states), 6 interior page templates (one per CEFR level, evolving handwriting style), La Rana perched on the journal edge.
