El Viaje del Jaguar

Composer & Sound Design Brief

A Spanish-learning game set in a mythological Colombia. 89 destinations, 3 worlds, 8 ecosystems. The student travels with Yaguará, a female jaguar spirit, restoring names to a world that is forgetting its language. The journey’s final answer is “Escuchar” — to listen.

01 Sound Philosophy

This game is about listening. Sound is not decoration — it is the subject.

The Core Idea

The world of the game is losing its names. Rivers flow but can’t say what they are. Birds sing but the songs carry no words. The student’s journey is to restore language to a world that is forgetting it.

This means sound must evolve with the student. At A1, the world is barely audible — a heartbeat, a single drop of water. By C2, the world is a symphony. The student’s progress literally fills the world with sound.

And then, at the very end, the most powerful thing of all: silence. Not empty silence, but silence that is full — because the student has learned to hear what was always there.

“Los sonidos están. Pero la música se fue.”
— Yaguará, destination 33 (the grey place)

Three Rules

1. Less is more. A single note placed well is worth more than a full orchestration. The game runs for 8,900 hours of content. The music must not exhaust the listener.

2. Colombian, not generic Latin. This game is set in Colombia. The sound palette should draw from vallenato, cumbia, bambuco, currulao, joropo — not generic “Latin guitar.” Indigenous instruments (gaita, maracón, quena, tambora) are welcome. But subtly — as texture, not performance.

3. Silence is content. Some destinations are about loss, forgetting, the absence of names. These moments need composed silence — not just “no music.” A room tone, a breath, a single sustained note fading to nothing. Silence must feel intentional.

Important: This is a language-learning game. Students will hear spoken Spanish (text-to-speech) constantly. Music must never compete with speech. All loops must work well at low volume, beneath dialogue. No vocals in background music. No lyrics.

Background music / Ambient loops .mp3 192 kbps Seamless loop points. Stereo. Aim for 60–120 seconds per loop.

Sound effects (UI, feedback) .mp3 128 kbps 0.3–2 seconds. Mono is fine. Tight trim, no silence padding.

File Naming Convention

{category}-{name}-{variant}.mp3

Examples: world-abajo-theme.mp3, eco-bosque-ambient.mp3, sfx-correct-01.mp3, escape-heartbeat-loop.mp3, char-yaguara-leitmotif.mp3, sting-arrival-a1.mp3

Loudness

All files normalized to –16 LUFS (integrated). The game engine controls volume dynamically. Deliver at consistent loudness so the engine can mix properly.

03 The Three Worlds

The game is divided into three worlds, each with a distinct sonic identity that deepens as the student progresses.

Mundo de Abajo — The World Below

Theme + Loop

Destinations 1–21 (A1–A2). The student awakens in a mythological underground river system. Yaguará is their only guide. Everything is new, strange, and gentle.

Sonic identity: Water. Stone. Breath. A single marimba note echoing off cave walls. The drip of condensation. A distant, barely audible heartbeat. Think: the sound of being inside the earth, before you know any words for it.

Instruments: Marimba de chonta (single notes, not melodies), water percussion, guacharaca (very soft), breathy flute (gaita hembra). Minimal. Sparse. Space between notes matters more than the notes.

Progression: At A1 (dest1–12), almost nothing — room tone + occasional single note. At A2 (dest13–21), a tentative melody begins to form, like someone remembering a song they almost forgot.

2 loops (A1 sparse + A2 emerging) 60–90 sec each Seamless

Mundo del Medio — The Middle World

Theme + Loop

Destinations 22–55 (B1–B2). The student emerges into the human world. A Colombian town. Conflict arrives: Don Próspero wants to “modernize” language, Candelaria guards the old words. There is debate, loss, and the grey place where names go to die.

Sonic identity: River current. Human voices (not literal — the texture of many people). Accordion fragments (vallenatera). The tension between cumbia rhythms and silence. A town that is alive but losing something.

Instruments: Accordion (diatonic, vallenato-style) in fragments, caja vallenata, guacharaca, acoustic guitar (tiple), occasional distant tambora. Conversations happen between instruments — call and response. Some dissonance creeps in at B2 as the world darkens.

Progression: At B1 (dest22–38), rhythmic and warm — a town plaza with music floating from doorways. At B2 (dest39–55), the music thins and fragments. By dest44 (“the grey place”), the music is nearly gone — only an echo of the accordion, as if heard through fog.

2 loops (B1 warm + B2 fading) 90–120 sec each Seamless

Mundo de Arriba — The World Above

Theme + Loop

Destinations 56–89 (C1–C2). The student ascends to the cosmic world. Language becomes poetic, philosophical, metalinguistic. Yaguará transforms into her final form. The student is no longer learning — they are creating.

Sonic identity: Wind across mountain peaks. Stellar resonance. The overtone series. A choir of all the sounds from below, heard from very far above. Think: the sound of seeing the entire journey from the summit.

Instruments: Quena (Andean flute, sustained notes), charango (arpeggiated, high), zampona (pan pipes, very distant), bowed strings (cello or viola, single sustained notes). The gaita from Mundo de Abajo returns, but now it sings a full phrase. Wind as instrument.

Progression: At C1 (dest56–72), spacious and reverent — the student hears the whole language at once. At C2 (dest73–89), the instruments gradually converge into a single chord. By dest88, only one sustained note remains. At dest89, silence — and then the rana sings.

2 loops (C1 spacious + C2 converging) 90–120 sec each Seamless
“Un acorde hecho de los ochenta y nueve destinos.”
— Destination 89 narrative (the finale)

04 Ecosystem Ambient Loops

8 Colombian ecosystems, each with a distinct natural soundscape. These play during ecosystem-variant games (?eco= parameter).

1. Bosque — Cloud Forest

Ambient Loop

Dense canopy. Filtered light. Moisture dripping from leaves. Birdsong (turpial, barranquero) layered at different depths. A stream far below. Insects at dusk. The forest breathes slowly.

60 sec loopSeamless

2. Costa — Caribbean Coast

Ambient Loop

Waves on sand (medium rhythm, not crashing). Seabirds (pelicans, frigatebirds). Distant cumbia — so distant it’s just the ghost of a tambora beat. Coconut palms rustling. Salt air you can almost hear.

60 sec loopSeamless

3. Desierto — La Guajira Desert

Ambient Loop

Wind over flat sand. Dry heat shimmer (high-frequency haze). A single Wayuu loom clicking in the distance. Silence that isn’t empty — it’s the silence of watching. Occasional goat bell, very far.

60 sec loopSeamless

4. Islas — San Andrés & Providencia

Ambient Loop

Coral reef shallows. Water lapping on a wooden dock. Creole music fragments (calypso hint). Reggae bass pulse so low it’s felt, not heard. Tropical birds (oropendola). A fishing boat engine puttering in the distance.

60 sec loopSeamless

5. Llanos — Eastern Plains

Ambient Loop

Immense open space. Wind through tall grass. A distant arpa llanera (single plucked notes, not a melody). Cattle egrets. The Orinoco river’s low hum. Joropo rhythm implied by insect cadence, not actual instruments. Vastness.

60 sec loopSeamless

6. Nevada — Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta

Ambient Loop

High altitude. Thin air. Wind is colder here — higher pitch, more sustained. A distant poporo (Kogi meditation gourd) tapping. Condor wings. Frozen stream trickling under ice. Sacred mountain stillness.

60 sec loopSeamless

7. Selva — Amazon / Chocó Rainforest

Ambient Loop

Maximum biodiversity = maximum sound density. Howler monkeys (distant). Cicadas at three different pitches. Rain frogs (dendrobates). Water everywhere — dripping, flowing, pooling. A marimba de chonta echoing from a Chocó village. Humid, alive, layered.

60 sec loopSeamless

8. Sierra — Andean Highlands

Ambient Loop

Páramo ecosystem. Wind over frailejones. A tiple playing two notes (bambuco feel). Church bell from a distant pueblo. The eucalyptus-scented cold. Hummingbird wings (colibrí). The stillness of altitude, but with life in it.

60 sec loopSeamless

05 Escape Room Ambience

89 escape rooms across 3 worlds. Each world has a signature ambient bed. These are tense, immersive, slightly mysterious — the student is solving puzzles in a closed space.

Heartbeat — Mundo de Abajo Escape Rooms

Ambient Loop

A1–A2 rooms. The student is inside the earth. The dominant sound is a low, slow heartbeat — as if the cave itself is alive. Occasional water drip. Resonant stone. The heartbeat should feel safe, not threatening — like being held inside something ancient.

30–60 sec loopSeamlessVery low frequency emphasis

River — Mundo del Medio Escape Rooms

Ambient Loop

B1–B2 rooms. The student is near water. A river current is the constant — medium flow, not rapids. Occasional fish splash. The creak of a wooden boat. Ink on paper (a soft scratching). Stories are written in these rooms. The river carries them.

30–60 sec loopSeamlessMid-frequency, flowing

Wind — Mundo de Arriba Escape Rooms

Ambient Loop

C1–C2 rooms. The student is in the sky. Wind is the constant — not howling, but the sustained, almost tonal wind of high altitude. Words float in this wind. Occasionally a distant voice says something unintelligible, as if language itself is the atmosphere. Ethereal. The most “silence-adjacent” of the three.

30–60 sec loopSeamlessHigh-frequency, spacious

Escape Room: Puzzle Solved

Sound Effect

A satisfying “click” or “unlock” sound when each puzzle is solved inside an escape room. Organic, not digital — like a stone sliding into place, or a lock carved from wood turning. Short, clean, rewarding.

1–2 sec3 variants (one per world)

Escape Room: Room Complete

Sting

All puzzles solved, the room opens. A door of light. A release of tension. Brief, uplifting, with a sense of emerging into open air. 3 variants, one per world — the Mundo de Arriba version should feel the most transcendent.

3–5 sec3 variants (one per world)

06 Character Leitmotifs

Not full themes — just a few notes that identify each character. These play briefly when the character speaks or appears. Think: a musical signature, 3–6 notes.

Yaguará — The Jaguar Spirit

Leitmotif

The protagonist and guide. Female jaguar. Wise, warm, sometimes playful. Evolves through 3 stages (Soñadora → Caminante → Guardiana). Her motif should be recognizable across all three but deepen — same intervals, richer voicing.

Instrument: Gaita hembra (breathy flute). At A1, a single phrase. At B1, harmonized with a second voice. At C1, the gaita is joined by strings.

3 variants (one per stage)3–5 sec each

Candelaria — The Guardian of Words

Leitmotif

Appears at dest14. An old woman who guards riddles and forgotten names. Her sound should be like opening an old book — papery, golden, warm. She carries the cuaderno (journal).

Instrument: Tiple (small Colombian guitar), fingerpicked. 4–5 notes, a descending pattern that resolves upward at the end — like a question that already knows its answer.

1 motif3–4 sec

Don Próspero — The Antagonist

Leitmotif

Appears at dest19. A persuasive town leader who believes language should be “efficient” and “modern.” Not evil — just wrong. His motif should be seductive, rhythmic, slightly too fast. The kind of music that makes you nod along before you realize you’re agreeing.

Instrument: Accordion (vallenato diatonic), a quick ascending phrase. Charming, then slightly dissonant on the last note.

1 motif3–4 sec

Abuela Ceiba — The Ancient Tree

Leitmotif

The ceiba tree, oldest voice in the world. Speaks rarely. When she does, everything listens. Her sound should be impossibly deep and slow — like the earth itself humming.

Instrument: Bowed cello on the lowest string, a single sustained note with very slow vibrato. Or a didgeridoo-like drone. Something that vibrates in your chest.

1 motif4–6 sec

Doña Asunción — The Elder

Leitmotif

Appears at dest9. A village elder who remembers the old ways. Patient, kind, slightly sad. Her sound is domestic and warm — a kitchen, a garden, a rocking chair.

Instrument: Marimba de chonta, 3 gentle notes. Like a grandmother humming while she works.

1 motif2–3 sec

La Rana sin Dientes — The Toothless Frog

Leitmotif

A frog who cannot speak. Appears as a ghost at dest10, gains substance through the journey, and at dest89 produces “un sonido diminuto — algo anterior a las palabras.” Her motif starts as silence (literally nothing) and, over 89 destinations, becomes a single, beautiful, tiny note.

Instrument: None at first. Then a single plucked note (thumb piano / kalimba). At dest89, this note blossoms into a brief, crystalline phrase — the first song of a creature who was always there but couldn’t speak.

3 variants: silence, single note, first song1–4 sec

07 Gameplay Sound Effects

Short, organic sounds for game interactions. These play hundreds of times — they must be pleasant without becoming annoying. Natural materials only: wood, water, stone, wind. No digital bleeps.

Correct Answer (Harmony)

Sound Effect

The world approves. A brief, warm resolution. Like a leaf landing on still water, or a bird answering a call. Satisfying but not celebratory — this is the world settling into rightness, not a game show buzzer.

0.5–1 sec3 variants for variety

Incorrect Answer (Desequilibrio)

Sound Effect

The world is slightly unsettled, not punishing. A soft “not quite” — like a stone that doesn’t quite fit its socket, or a bird call that trails off unresolved. Gentle. The student should feel invited to try again, not scolded.

0.5–1 sec3 variants

Encounter Start

Sound Effect

A new game encounter appears. A breath. An opening. Like turning a page in a book, or a door of light appearing. Very subtle — this plays before every single game.

0.3–0.8 sec1 version

Destination Complete

Sting

All encounters in a destination finished. The “world restored” moment. A brief swell of the current world theme — a musical exhale. Warm, earned, not triumphant. The student has given this place its name back.

3–5 sec3 variants (one per world)

Phase Transition (Breathe)

Sound Effect

Between game phases within a destination, the engine shows a “breathe” screen. This needs a transitional sound — a shift, a deepening. Like descending one level deeper into a cenote. Not dramatic, just: “something changed.”

2–3 sec1 version

Drag & Drop

Sound Effect

Picking up a word chip (soft wooden click), dragging (nothing — silence), dropping into a blank (a satisfying “fit” sound, like a puzzle piece). If wrong drop: a soft “miss” (same as desequilibrio but shorter).

Pick: 0.2 sec / Drop: 0.3 sec1 set

Voice Mode: Listening

Sound Effect

The microphone activates and the game is listening. A soft, sustained tone — like a tuning fork being held in the air. Should feel like the world is leaning in to hear the student speak. Ends with a soft release when recording stops.

Start: 0.5 sec / End: 0.3 sec1 set

Pair Match

Sound Effect

Two cards matched in a pair game. A satisfying “click together” — like two magnets finding each other. Brief, clean, slightly musical (an interval — perhaps a fifth).

0.4 sec1 version

9b Voice Cloning Approach

Character voices will be generated via voice cloning, not traditional voice recording. The composer’s role is to provide the source material.

What We Need from the Composer

Instead of recording line-by-line dialogue, we need high-quality voice samples for cloning. The AI voice system will generate all spoken lines from these samples. The composer’s deliverable is the raw voice material, not the final dialogue.

Per character voice: 5–10 minutes of clean, expressive speech in the character’s register. Read provided sample texts that cover the character’s emotional range (warmth, authority, wonder, sadness, humor). Record in a treated space with minimal reverb.

Format: 48 kHz WAV, 24-bit, mono. No processing, no compression, no EQ. We need the cleanest possible source for cloning.

9c “Mi aventura por Colombia” — Travel Narrative Theme

A new system where the student’s writing accumulates into a personal travel narrative across 89 real Colombian landmarks. This needs its own musical identity.

Aventura Theme — Writing Motif

Theme + Loop

A gentle, intimate theme that plays when the student enters the “Mi aventura por Colombia” tab in the cuaderno (journal). This is the student’s personal space — their travel chronicle. The sound should evoke handwriting by lantern light, the feeling of a traveler’s journal at the end of the day. Not the game’s mythological world, but the real Colombia — the sound of the student mapping their own journey.

Instruments: Tiple (fingerpicked, gentle), bambuco rhythm implied but not stated, a soft guacharaca keeping time like a heartbeat. Perhaps a melodica or harmonica (traveler’s instrument). Warm, personal, diaristic.

60–90 sec loopSeamlessMust work under typing/reading

Landmark Arrival Chime

Sound Effect

When a student completes a destination and “arrives” at a new Colombian landmark on their travel map, a brief chime plays. Each of the 8 ecosystems gets a variation: sierra = tiple note, bosque = bird call, costa = wave + marimba, desierto = wind chime, islas = steel drum tap, llanos = arpa pluck, nevada = crystal bell, selva = rain stick.

10 The Finale (Destination 89)

This is the most important sound in the entire game. Everything has led here.

“Un sonido diminuto… no es una palabra… no es una canción… algo anterior a las palabras.”
— The moment the rana sin dientes produces her first sound

The 89-Destination Chord

Composed Piece

All 89 destinations form a single chord. This is not metaphorical — we need to hear it. A gradual accumulation of every sonic element from the journey: the water drop from dest1, the heartbeat, the marimba note, the gaita, the accordion, the river, the cumbia ghost, the wind, the quena, the silence of dest44 (a gap in the chord), the cello, the charango — all converging into one sustained, beautiful, impossible chord.

It should take about 15–20 seconds to fully form. Then it sustains for 5 seconds. Then it fades — not to silence, but to the sound of a single heartbeat. The same heartbeat from dest1. We have come full circle.

20–30 secThe single most important audio deliverable

La Rana’s First Sound

Composed Moment

After the chord fades, after the heartbeat — silence. Real silence. 3 seconds of nothing. And then: a tiny, crystalline note. La rana sin dientes, who has been silent for 89 destinations, produces her first sound. It is not a word. It is not a song. It is something before words — the impulse to name.

Instrument: Kalimba, single note. Or a glass harmonica. Something that sounds like it has been waiting inside glass for a long time. Pure. Tiny. Alive.

3 sec silence + 2 sec noteMust be the most delicate sound in the entire game

The Naming Ceremony

Composed Moment

The student names the rana. This is the culmination of the entire riddle quest. After the student types the name and presses “Nombrar,” the world responds. Every leitmotif plays its first note simultaneously — Yaguará, Candelaria, Ceiba, even Don Próspero — as if the whole world is saying the name together. Then: the rana’s kalimba note, now harmonized. She has a name. She can sing.

8–12 secMust feel like the world inhaling together

11 What This Is NOT

NOT a video game soundtrack. No epic orchestration. No battle music. No victory fanfares. This is a language-learning journey about listening, naming, and silence. The music serves the learning.

NOT generic “world music.” No pan flutes from Peru, no mariachi from Mexico, no tango from Argentina. This is Colombia. Vallenato, cumbia, bambuco, currulao, joropo. If you don’t know these genres, research them before composing.

NOT background wallpaper. Every sound must be intentional. If a moment doesn’t need music, it shouldn’t have music. Silence is a valid (and often superior) choice. The game’s finale literally resolves to silence.

NOT vocals. No singing, no humming, no vocalization of any kind in background music. The only voice the student should hear is the TTS reading Spanish. Music with vocals will compete with comprehension.

NOT loud. Everything must work at very low volume, under dialogue. The student is reading, listening to speech, and concentrating on language patterns. Music is the air in the room, not the conversation.

Important — Please Discuss Before Starting

Before composing, please read the narrative files (provided separately) for dest1, dest44, and dest89. These three destinations define the emotional arc: birth → loss → restoration. If the music doesn’t serve this arc, nothing else matters.

The game’s final answer is “Escuchar” — to listen. The student who reaches dest89 has spent hundreds of hours learning to hear. Honor that by composing music that rewards careful listening.

New in this version: The course now spans 89 destinations (up from 58), maps to 89 real Colombian landmarks, and features literary peaks from 9 authors (Pombo, Rivera, Carrasquilla, Macías, Mejía, Obeso, Acosta, Whitman, and Poe). The “Mi aventura por Colombia” system adds a personal writing dimension — every student creates their own travel chronicle. Voice cloning replaces traditional voice recording.
